In Veritasium video, Derek says that, assuming the stock prices behave as a random walk at every time step, the price distribution follows a normal distribution with dispersing std. dev., which follows the heat dispersion equation. I will demonstrate that here.

Stock prices can be modeled as a 1-D Brownian motion. That is, at every time step, the stock prices go either up or down unit with equal probability.

def BrownianMotion(nSteps, initial_price, dt=1, mu=0, sigma=0.1):
    """
    dt: time increment
    mu: Drift coefficient (average change in price per unit time)
    sigma: Diffusion coefficient (std. dev., volatility), the measure of how unpredictable the price is
    Returns:  numpy arrays of len=nSteps, including the initial price at t=0.
    """
    incs = np.random.normal(loc=mu*dt, scale=sigma*np.sqrt(dt), size=nSteps-1)
    prices = np.concatenate(([initial_price],initial_price+np.cumsum(incs)))
    ts = np.linspace(0, nSteps*dt, nSteps)
    return ts, prices

nTrials = 5000
nSteps = 1000 # each step is 1 unit of time.
initial_price = 100
n_to_draw = 10
TRIAL_RESULTS = np.zeros((nTrials, nSteps))
for t in range(nTrials):
    ts, prices = BrownianMotion(nSteps, initial_price)
    TRIAL_RESULTS[t,:] = prices

plt.figure(figsize=(8, 4))
for prices in TRIAL_RESULTS[::int(nTrials/n_to_draw)]:
    plt.plot(ts, prices, lw=1)
plt.title("1D Brownian Motion (Wiener Process)")
plt.xlabel("Time")
plt.ylabel("Stock price")
plt.ylim([95,105])
plt.grid(True)
plt.axhline(y=100, color='red', linestyle='--', label='original price')
plt.legend()
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

where $x=$ stock price and $u=$ number density.
